# Utah Property Tax Appeal Process: Complete Reference

**Research Date:** April 2026

---

## Overview

Utah property tax appeals follow a structured, multi-level process. The procedure is **largely the same for residential and commercial property** at the procedural level — same forms, same deadlines, same hearing bodies. The critical differences are in **what evidence you bring** and **which valuation approaches apply**. A third track exists for centrally assessed properties (utilities, mines, telecoms) which is entirely separate.

There are three categories of property in Utah's system:

| Category | Assessed By | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Locally Assessed Real Property** | County Assessor | Homes, apartments, office buildings, retail, vacant land |
| **Locally Assessed Personal Property** | County Assessor (owner-reported) | Business equipment, furniture, fixtures, machinery |
| **Centrally Assessed Property** | Utah State Tax Commission | Utilities, mines, telecoms, railroads, pipelines |

---

## LOCALLY ASSESSED PROPERTY (Residential + Commercial)

This is the track that covers both residential homes and commercial buildings. The process is identical — the evidence differs.

---

### Phase 1: Assessment and Notice

**What happens:** The county assessor values every property at fair market value as of the **January 1 lien date** each year. Counties use 5-year cyclical reappraisal schedules combined with computer-assisted mass appraisal (CAMA) systems to update values annually.

**When you're notified:** Valuation notices are mailed in **late July to early August** (varies by county). The notice shows:
- Current year's assessed market value
- Prior year's value
- Breakdown of land vs. improvement value
- Tax rate and estimated tax amount
- Appeal instructions and deadline

**First step for any owner:** Request the county assessor's property record card. Check for factual errors:
- Square footage (building and lot)
- Number of bedrooms/bathrooms
- Year built
- Condition rating
- Any features you don't actually have

Factual errors are the easiest wins. If the assessor has your 1,800 sq ft home recorded as 2,100 sq ft, that's a straightforward correction.

---

### Phase 2: County Board of Equalization (BOE) Appeal

This is the **first formal appeal** and is mandatory before escalating to the state.

#### Deadline

**The later of:**
- **September 15**, OR
- **45 days from the mailing date** of the valuation notice

If September 15 falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day. **Missing this deadline forfeits your right to appeal for the current tax year.**

#### Late Appeals (Limited)

Late appeals may be accepted until **March 31 of the following tax year**, but only under narrow circumstances:
- Death in the family
- Dire medical emergency
- County failed to send proper notification
- Factual errors in county records
- Extraordinary, unanticipated circumstances

The BOE must accept the late petition before it will be reviewed.

#### How to File

Each county has its own process, but generally:

| Method | Details |
|---|---|
| **Online** | Most Wasatch Front counties have electronic filing portals |
| **Mail** | Download and complete the county's appeal form (e.g., BE01 in Salt Lake County) |
| **In person** | County auditor's office; some have drop boxes |

**What the form requires:**
- Parcel number (from your valuation notice)
- Property address
- County's assessed value
- Your proposed value
- Basis for appeal
- Supporting evidence/documentation

**Important:** You can only challenge the **total market value** for the current year. You cannot separately appeal just the land value or just the improvement value.

#### Representation

- You may represent yourself
- You may authorize a representative (attorney, consultant, agent)
- If using a representative, **written authorization must be submitted** with the appeal
- File separate appeals per parcel (unless contiguous parcels with identical appeal basis)

### Evidence Requirements: RESIDENTIAL vs. COMMERCIAL

This is where the two tracks diverge significantly.

#### Residential Property Evidence

The **sales comparison approach** is the primary (and usually only) valuation method accepted for residential property.

| Evidence Type | What to Provide | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Comparable sales** | 3-5 properties sold within 12 months prior to January 1 lien date | MLS "Residential Full Reports" preferred. Properties should match in age, location, size, quality, style, and condition. More similar comps carry more weight. |
| **Your own purchase** | Closing/settlement statement from an arm's-length transaction within 12 months of Jan 1 | Must be between unrelated parties with no duress |
| **Professional appraisal** | Independent fee appraisal with January 1 valuation date | Most effective evidence, but costs $400+. Cost-benefit analysis recommended. |
| **Factual errors** | Documentation showing incorrect property records | Square footage, room count, lot size, condition |
| **Natural disaster damage** | Repair bids/receipts showing damage exceeding 30% of taxable value | Photos and contractor estimates |
| **Access disruption** | Evidence of decreased value from road construction, traffic changes | Less common but valid |

**What does NOT work for residential:**
- The income approach (rent income) **cannot** be used for single-family homes, condos, or basement apartments — even if they are rented out
- Cap rates from outside the immediate area are generally not accepted
- Emotional arguments ("I can't afford this") are not evidence of market value
- Comparable sales from outside the local market area carry little weight

#### Commercial Property Evidence

Commercial properties can use **multiple valuation approaches**, making appeals both more complex and potentially more powerful.

| Valuation Approach | When It Applies | What to Provide |
|---|---|---|
| **Sales comparison** | All commercial property | 3-5 comparable commercial sales within 12 months of Jan 1; full transaction details |
| **Income approach** | Income-producing property: offices, retail, industrial, apartments (2+ units) | See detailed requirements below |
| **Cost approach** | Special-purpose properties, new construction | Replacement cost less depreciation |

**Income Approach — Required Documentation:**

| Document | Details |
|---|---|
| **Rent rolls** | Prior year ending December 31; all units, tenants, lease terms, rental rates |
| **Income & expense statements** | For each of the **3 years prior** to the current tax year |
| **Vacancy rates** | Actual vacancy history plus market vacancy comparables |
| **Operating expenses** | All categories: management, maintenance, insurance, utilities, taxes |
| **Capitalization rate** | Must be derived from **local market data**. Cap rates from outside the immediate area may be rejected. |
| **Lease abstracts** | For properties with significant tenants; terms, escalations, options |

**For owner-occupied commercial property:** You may submit equivalent data from comparable commercial properties, with evidence justifying similarities and differences.

**What does NOT work for commercial:**
- Income data alone without supporting comparable sales
- Cap rates sourced from national surveys without local adjustment
- Personal property income mixed with real property income

#### Summary: Evidence by Property Type

| Property Type | Primary Approach | Secondary Approach | Key Documents |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single-family home | Sales comparison | N/A (income not allowed) | 3-5 comps, own purchase docs, or appraisal |
| Condo/townhome | Sales comparison | N/A | Same as above |
| Duplex/triplex/fourplex | Sales comparison | Income approach allowed | Comps + rent rolls + operating statements |
| Apartment complex (5+) | Income approach | Sales comparison | 3 years income/expense + rent rolls + cap rate |
| Office/retail/industrial | Income approach | Sales comparison, cost | 3 years income/expense + rent rolls + cap rate |
| Vacant land | Sales comparison | N/A | Comparable land sales |
| Special purpose | Cost approach | Sales comparison | Replacement cost analysis |

### The BOE Hearing

#### Format
- **Informal** — not a courtroom proceeding
- A county hearing officer conducts the hearing
- Both the property owner and the assessor's office present evidence
- Oral testimony is accepted
- **Attendance is optional** — you may submit written statements and evidence without appearing

#### What the Hearing Officer Does
- Reviews all submitted evidence from both sides
- Considers accuracy, reliability, and comparability of evidence
- Issues a market value recommendation to the Board
- The Board votes to approve, modify, or reject the recommendation

#### Timeline
- Hearings are typically scheduled **August through early October**
- Varies significantly by county based on appeal volume
- Salt Lake County targets completion by October 1
- **Decision is mailed in writing** after Board approval
- Taxes remain due **November 30** regardless of pending appeal

#### Key Legal Points
- **The assessed value is presumed correct.** The burden of proof is on you to show it's wrong.
- The Board considers **market value** only — not the tax amount, not ability to pay, not fairness relative to neighbors.
- For residential/condo properties, you receive **30 days** to review the hearing officer's recommendation after Board approval.

---

### Phase 3: Utah State Tax Commission Appeal

If the BOE decision is unfavorable, you can escalate to the state level.

#### Deadline

**30 days from the date of the BOE decision.** Non-negotiable.

#### How to File

- Submit **Form TC-194** (Request for Redetermination of County Board of Equalization Decision) to the **County Auditor** (not directly to the Tax Commission)
- The County Auditor forwards the complete appeal record to the Tax Commission Appeals Unit

**Filing methods:**
- Email: tax-appeals@utah.gov
- Mail: Utah State Tax Commission Appeals Unit, 1950 West 210 North, Salt Lake City, UT 84134
- Fax: 801-297-3919

#### Evidence Rules

- All evidence must be submitted **10 business days prior** to any hearing
- Evidence should address **market value as of the January 1 lien date**
- New evidence not presented at the BOE level may be introduced

#### Three Resolution Paths

**Path 1: Mediation (Most Common)**
- Voluntary process with an Administrative Law Judge or Tax Commissioner as mediator
- You and the county representatives negotiate toward a settlement
- **The majority of Tax Commission appeals are resolved through mediation**
- If mediation fails, proceeds to Initial Hearing

**Path 2: Initial Hearing**
- Informal proceeding before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ)
- Both sides present testimony and evidence
- ALJ issues a written decision
- Either party may request a Formal Hearing within 30 days if dissatisfied

**Path 3: Formal Hearing**
- Recorded proceeding with sworn testimony
- Cross-examination permitted
- Full legal procedures apply
- ALJ (or Tax Commissioners for complex cases) issues final written decision
- Parties may waive the Initial Hearing and proceed directly to Formal Hearing

#### Beyond the Tax Commission

- Either party may seek **judicial review** of the Tax Commission's final order within 30 days
- This goes to Utah District Court

## CENTRALLY ASSESSED PROPERTY (Separate Track)

This track applies to a narrow category of large, multi-county operations. Included here for completeness but **not relevant to a residential protest business**.

### What's Centrally Assessed
- Mining operations
- Utilities (electric, gas, water)
- Telecommunications companies
- Transportation companies (railroads, pipelines)
- Any property operating across county lines

### Key Differences from Local Assessment

| Feature | Locally Assessed | Centrally Assessed |
|---|---|---|
| Assessed by | County Assessor | Utah State Tax Commission Property Tax Division |
| Owner reporting | Not required (assessor inspects) | Self-reporting affidavits due **March 1** |
| Assessment notices | Late July/August | Mailed by **May 1** |
| Appeal deadline | September 15 | **August 1** |
| Appeal filed with | County Board of Equalization | Utah State Tax Commission directly |
| Who can appeal | Property owner | Property owner **and** affected counties |
| Hearing timeline | Aug-Oct | Scheduling conference in **early November** |
| Resolution | BOE decision, then optional Tax Commission appeal | Direct to Tax Commission ALJ |

### Centrally Assessed Appeal Process

1. **March 1** — Owner submits self-reporting affidavit
2. **May 1** — Tax Commission mails assessment notice
3. **August 1** — Appeal deadline (owner or counties)
4. **Early November** — Scheduling conference with ALJ; discovery and exchange of information ordered
5. **Settlement negotiations** — Most cases resolve here (three-party agreements between owner, county, and Tax Commission; or two-party agreements)
6. **Initial Hearing** — If no settlement; informal proceeding
7. **Formal Hearing** — If either party disagrees with Initial Hearing decision
8. **Judicial Review** — Within 30 days of final order

## PERSONAL PROPERTY (Business Equipment/Fixtures)

Business personal property (equipment, furniture, fixtures, machinery) follows its own rules within the locally assessed framework.

### Assessment Method
- Owners file self-assessment affidavits annually
- Counties apply **depreciation schedules** to reported cost
- The depreciation schedule is the primary valuation tool — challenging it with income data is generally not permitted

### Appeal Process
- Same BOE process as real property
- **30-day appeal window** after assessment notice
- Evidence should focus on: actual condition, obsolescence, remaining useful life
- Income-based arguments are weak for personal property

## Complete Timeline Summary

### Residential / Commercial (Locally Assessed)

| Date | Action | Who |
|---|---|---|
| **January 1** | Lien date — all values assessed as of this date | County Assessor |
| **Late July - August** | Valuation notices mailed | County Assessor |
| **August 1 - September 15** | Primary appeal window (45 days from notice or Sept 15, whichever is later) | Property Owner |
| **August - October** | BOE hearings conducted | County BOE |
| **October** | BOE decisions mailed | County BOE |
| **November 30** | Property taxes due (regardless of pending appeal) | Property Owner |
| **Within 30 days of BOE decision** | Tax Commission appeal deadline (Form TC-194) | Property Owner |
| **30 days before hearing** | Tax Commission hearing scheduled | Tax Commission |
| **10 business days before hearing** | All evidence must be submitted | Both parties |
| **March 31 (following year)** | Late appeal deadline (restricted criteria) | Property Owner |

### Centrally Assessed

| Date | Action | Who |
|---|---|---|
| **March 1** | Self-reporting affidavits due | Property Owner |
| **May 1** | Assessment notices mailed | Tax Commission |
| **August 1** | Appeal deadline | Owner or County |
| **Early November** | Scheduling conference | Tax Commission ALJ |
| **Ongoing** | Settlement negotiations, hearings | All parties |

## Operational Implications for a Protest Business

### What This Process Means for a Service Provider

**Residential clients:**
- You need MLS access for comparable sales data — this is the entire case
- Appraisals are the strongest evidence but cost $400+, often exceeding the expected savings on moderate-value homes
- The work is formulaic: pull comps, build a comparison grid, present to hearing officer
- Can be systematized and scaled with data tools

**Commercial clients:**
- Requires financial analysis skills — income approach, cap rate derivation, expense normalization
- 3 years of financial data needed from the client
- Higher value per client but more labor-intensive
- Needs local cap rate expertise (national data gets rejected)

**Seasonality is extreme:**
- Notices arrive late July
- All filing must happen by September 15
- Hearings run August through October
- Revenue hits Q4
- The rest of the year is marketing and preparation

**No special license required** to represent someone at a BOE hearing — only written authorization from the property owner. Attorney representation is optional, not required.
