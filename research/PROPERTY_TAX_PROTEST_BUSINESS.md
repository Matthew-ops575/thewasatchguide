# Utah Property Tax Protest Business: Viability Analysis

**Research Date:** April 2026
**Status:** Exploratory / Go-No-Go Decision

---

## Executive Summary

A residential property tax protest business in Utah is **viable but constrained** compared to the same model in Texas. Utah's low effective tax rates (0.55% median) mean smaller per-client revenue, but rapidly rising home values, a favorable appeal success rate (~70%), and **virtually no dedicated residential competition** create a legitimate first-mover window. The biggest risk isn't demand — it's unit economics. This analysis concludes with a conditional recommendation: pursue it only as a **lightweight, tech-enabled operation** with minimal fixed costs, or bolt it onto an existing consulting practice as a lead-generation channel.

## 1. Utah Property Tax Landscape

### Tax Rates and Burden

| Metric | Utah | National Median |
|---|---|---|
| Median effective tax rate | 0.55% | 1.02% |
| Median annual tax bill | $2,534 | $2,400 |
| Median home value | ~$459,000 | ~$281,000 |
| Tax burden ranking | 14th highest overall | — |
| Property tax rate ranking | 5th lowest | — |

**Key insight:** Utah's property tax *rate* is low, but because home values are high, the *dollar amount* paid is close to the national median. The rate alone is misleading — homeowners still feel the pain in their actual bill.

### Why the Problem is Getting Worse

- **Home values tripled in a decade**: Taxable value of Utah homes went from ~$126B to ~$375B over the past 10 years, while only ~200,000 new residential properties were added.
- **Legislative "perfect storm"**: In 2018, the Utah Legislature froze the basic levy tax rate for five years to boost education funding. This meant that as values surged (especially 2021-2023), homeowners absorbed the full impact without the normal truth-in-taxation rate adjustments.
- **Assessment shock is real**: A $350,000 home can suddenly be assessed at $420,000+ after nearby sales push market values higher — and the tax increase is automatic, no rate vote required.
- **Vulnerable populations hit hardest**: Retirees on fixed incomes, single-parent households, and long-term residents in legacy neighborhoods report cutting back on essentials or withdrawing savings to cover escrow shortages.
- **Salt Lake County specifically**: Approved a 14.65% property tax increase for 2026 budget, adding ~$6/month for the average homeowner on a $638K home.

### County Variation (Selected)

| County | Median Tax Bill | Effective Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Summit | $4,797 | High (resort values) |
| Salt Lake | ~$2,500+ | ~0.67% |
| Utah | ~$2,200 | ~0.55% |
| Carbon | $711 | Low |
| Uintah | — | 0.98% (highest) |
| Rich | — | 0.42% (lowest) |

**Takeaway:** The addressable market is concentrated along the Wasatch Front (Salt Lake, Utah, Davis, Weber counties), where home values and tax bills are highest.

## 2. The Appeal Process in Utah

### How It Works

1. **County Assessor** sets market value (as of January 1 lien date)
2. **Notice of Valuation** mailed to homeowners (typically July-August)
3. **Board of Equalization (BOE) appeal** — must be filed by **September 15**
4. **Late appeals** accepted until March 31 (stricter criteria)
5. **State Tax Commission appeal** — if BOE decision is unfavorable, 30-day window to escalate

### What Makes a Strong Appeal

- Independent appraisal (within 12-18 months of lien date)
- Comparable sales data showing lower values
- Factual errors: wrong square footage, incorrect lot size, missing condition issues
- The assessed value is **presumed correct** unless the homeowner provides evidence otherwise

### Success Rates and Outcomes

- **~70-80% of appeals succeed** when properly documented (Weber County data)
- Successful appeals typically yield a **10-15% reduction** in assessed value
- Every $1,000 reduction in market value saves **$4-$8 in annual taxes** (varies by district)

### Worked Example

- Home assessed at $550,000
- 12% reduction via appeal = $66,000 value reduction
- Tax savings at 0.55% rate = ~$363/year
- At 40% contingency fee = **$145 revenue per client**

This is the core unit economics challenge. Compare to Texas, where a similar reduction on a home taxed at 2%+ would yield 3-4x the revenue per client.

## 4. Competitive Landscape

### Direct Competitors in Utah

| Competitor | Focus | Pricing | Threat Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Preston Property Tax Consultants** | Commercial (primarily). Since 1983. Multi-state. | Not publicly listed; likely flat fee or % of savings for commercial | **Low for residential** — they serve REITs and developers, not homeowners |
| **O'Connor & Associates** | National. All property types. | Contingency (typically 30-50% of savings) | **Medium** — they operate in Utah but their scale means generic service, not local expertise |
| **ExcelAppraise** | Utah County appraisals | Appraisal fee (~$300-500) | **Low** — they sell appraisals, not end-to-end protest service |
| **Local attorneys/CPAs** | Ad hoc, not specialized | Hourly ($200-400/hr) | **Low** — expensive, not contingency-based |

### National Players Not Yet in Utah

| Company | Model | Likelihood of Utah Entry |
|---|---|---|
| **Ownwell** | Tech-first, 25% contingency, $50M raised | **High** — expanding nationally, launched national appeals packet |
| **Home Tax Shield** | $30/yr + 30% contingency, Texas only | **Low** — Texas-focused |
| **TurboProtest** | Tech-enabled, Texas-focused | **Low** — Texas-focused |

### Competitive Assessment

**The residential Utah market is essentially unserved.** There is no dedicated, consumer-friendly, contingency-based residential property tax protest company focused on Utah. This is both an opportunity and a warning — it could mean untapped demand, or it could mean the unit economics don't justify a dedicated operation.

**Why would a customer choose you over existing options?**

1. **vs. doing it themselves**: Most homeowners don't know they can appeal, don't know the deadline (Sept 15), and don't have access to comp data. You remove all friction.
2. **vs. O'Connor**: Local expertise, personal service, Utah-specific comp data. O'Connor is a machine — you're a specialist.
3. **vs. Preston**: They don't serve residential customers. You fill the gap.
4. **vs. hiring an attorney**: Contingency fee = no upfront cost. Attorneys charge hourly whether they win or not.
5. **vs. Ownwell (if they enter)**: First-mover advantage, local brand, Wasatch Front expertise, in-person hearing representation.

## 5. Business Model Analysis

### Revenue Model: Contingency Fee

The industry standard is **contingency-based**: no savings, no fee.

| Fee Structure | Revenue per Client | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 40% of first-year savings | $100-250 (typical) | Utah's low rates compress this |
| 50% of first-year savings | $125-315 | Aggressive but defensible for no-risk model |
| Flat fee ($150-200) | $150-200 (guaranteed) | Higher per-client but lower conversion |

**Realistic scenario at scale:**
- Average home value: $500,000
- Average successful reduction: 10% ($50,000)
- Tax savings at 0.55%: $275/year
- Revenue at 40% contingency: **$110 per successful client**
- At 70% success rate, revenue per enrolled client: **$77**

### Volume Requirements

| Annual Revenue Target | Clients Needed (at $110/success) | Enrolled Clients (at 70% success) |
|---|---|---|
| $25,000 (side income) | 227 | 325 |
| $50,000 (part-time) | 455 | 650 |
| $100,000 (full-time) | 909 | 1,300 |

Getting to 1,300 enrolled clients as a solo operation is ambitious but not impossible if acquisition costs are low.

### Cost Structure

| Cost Category | Estimated Annual |
|---|---|
| LLC formation + annual report | $77 first year, $18 ongoing |
| Comp data subscriptions (MLS access, county data) | $1,200-3,000 |
| Website + marketing | $2,000-5,000 |
| E&O insurance | $1,000-2,000 |
| CRM/operations software | $500-1,500 |
| Appraisal costs (if needed for evidence) | Variable |
| **Total fixed overhead** | **$5,000-12,000/year** |

### Unit Economics Comparison: Utah vs. Texas

| Metric | Utah | Texas |
|---|---|---|
| Median home value | $459,000 | $238,000 |
| Effective tax rate | 0.55% | 1.60% |
| Median annual tax bill | $2,534 | $3,808 |
| 10% value reduction savings | ~$250 | ~$380 |
| Revenue at 40% contingency | ~$100 | ~$152 |
| Revenue at higher values (Utah $600K / TX $400K) | ~$132 | ~$256 |

**Utah produces roughly 40-65% of the per-client revenue that Texas does.** This is the fundamental constraint. You need more clients or lower costs to make the same money.

### Break-Even Analysis

At $8,000 annual fixed costs and $110 average revenue per successful client:
- **Break-even: 73 successful appeals** (~104 enrolled at 70% success rate)
- This is achievable in year one with modest marketing

## 6. Market Sizing

### Total Addressable Market (TAM)

- ~1.22M housing units in Utah
- ~70% owner-occupied = ~855,000 owner-occupied homes
- Wasatch Front concentration (Salt Lake, Utah, Davis, Weber) = ~500,000 homes

### Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

- Homes assessed above $400K (where savings justify effort): ~60% = ~300,000 homes
- Homeowners who would consider an appeal if presented with the option: ~10-15% = 30,000-45,000

### Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)

- Year 1 realistic capture: 0.1-0.3% of SAM = **300-900 enrolled clients**
- Year 3 with brand establishment: 1-2% = **3,000-6,000 enrolled clients**
- At $110/successful client: **Year 1 revenue $23K-$69K / Year 3 revenue $231K-$462K**

## 7. SWOT Analysis

### Strengths
- No dedicated residential competitor in Utah
- Contingency model removes customer risk
- Utah's 70-80% appeal success rate is strong
- Rising home values = rising assessment frustration
- Low startup costs

### Weaknesses
- Low effective tax rate compresses per-client revenue
- September 15 deadline creates extreme seasonality (all revenue in Q3-Q4)
- No licensing moat — easy to replicate
- Requires comp data expertise and hearing experience

### Opportunities
- First-mover advantage before Ownwell expands to Utah
- Legislative changes (rate freezes, tax increases) fuel demand
- Commercial upsell path (higher per-client revenue)
- Partnership with real estate agents, mortgage brokers for referrals
- Content marketing / SEO (property tax guides for Utah homeowners)
- Annual recurring revenue — reassessments happen every year

### Threats
- Ownwell ($74M funded) enters Utah and undercuts on price
- Utah Legislature reforms assessment process, reducing appeal volume
- Low per-client revenue makes customer acquisition cost critical
- Property value corrections reduce overassessment gap

## 8. Strategic Recommendations

### If Pursuing: Lean Launch Model

1. **Start as a side operation.** The unit economics don't justify quitting a day job in year one.
2. **Target high-value properties first.** Summit County ($1M+ homes), upper Salt Lake County, Draper/Alpine/Highland. A $1M home with a 10% reduction saves ~$550/year = $220 fee at 40%. Much better than average.
3. **Build a simple intake website.** Address lookup, instant estimate of potential savings, one-click enrollment. This is what Ownwell does well.
4. **Leverage county data.** Utah's county assessor data is publicly available. Build or buy tools to identify overassessed properties proactively and do outbound marketing to those homeowners.
5. **Create content.** "How to Appeal Your Property Tax in Salt Lake County" — this is SEO-friendly, builds authority, and captures leads.
6. **Charge 40-50% contingency.** The market will bear it because there's no alternative, and the customer pays nothing if you fail.
7. **Partner with real estate agents.** They have the client relationships and the comp data. Referral fee or co-marketing arrangement.
8. **Plan for seasonality.** Revenue concentrates in Sept-Dec. Use Jan-Aug for marketing, content creation, and commercial prospecting.

### If Not Pursuing: Why You'd Walk Away

- **$77 average revenue per enrolled client** is thin. One bad marketing channel burns through margin fast.
- **Extreme seasonality** — essentially a 3-4 month business.
- **Ownwell is coming.** With $74M in funding and a national expansion strategy, they could enter Utah within 12-18 months and compete on technology and price.
- **No moat.** The business is pure execution — anyone with comp data and hearing experience can replicate it.
- **Better uses of time.** If you have an existing consulting practice generating higher per-hour revenue, this may not clear the bar.

---

## 9. Verdict

### Score: 6/10 — Conditionally Viable

**The demand is real.** People are spending money on this problem nationally (Ownwell's $50M raise, O'Connor's $213M in client savings, 900K protests/year in Texas alone). Utah's rising home values and assessment frustration are creating genuine consumer pain, particularly along the Wasatch Front.

**The gap is real.** No one is serving Utah residential homeowners with a dedicated, contingency-based protest service.

**But the math is tight.** Utah's low effective tax rate means you need significantly more volume than a Texas operator to generate the same revenue. The business works if you can acquire clients cheaply (content marketing, referral partnerships, outbound to overassessed properties) and keep overhead minimal.

**Best-case path:** Launch lean, prove unit economics with 200-300 clients in year one, and either scale or sell/partner before Ownwell enters the market. Worst case, you've built a useful content asset and local expertise that has value in other ways.

**This is not a "build a company" opportunity. It's a "test a hypothesis cheaply" opportunity.** Treat it accordingly.
